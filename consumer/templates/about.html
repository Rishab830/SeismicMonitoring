üìö Understanding Seismic Monitoring

A beginner's guide to earthquake detection and seismic wave analysis

üåä What is Seismic Monitoring?

Seismic monitoring is the science of detecting and measuring ground vibrations caused by earthquakes, volcanic activity, or other seismic events. Scientists use special instruments called seismometers placed around the world to continuously record these vibrations.

When an earthquake occurs anywhere on Earth, the energy travels through the planet as waves. These waves are detected by seismometers at monitoring stations, creating recordings called seismograms that scientists analyze to understand the earthquake's location, magnitude, and characteristics.

üìà Understanding the Seismogram (The Graph)

The colorful graph you see on the dashboard is called a seismogram. It shows how the ground moved over time at a specific location. Think of it like a heart monitor for the Earth!

How to Read the Graph:

  • Horizontal Axis (X-axis): Represents time - shows when movements occurred
  • Vertical Axis (Y-axis): Represents ground displacement - how much the ground moved up/down or side-to-side
  • Wavy Lines: Each line represents a different component of motion (North-South, East-West, Up-Down)
  • Colored Vertical Lines: Mark detected seismic phases (P-waves and S-waves)
üí° What You're Looking At:

A flat line means the ground is stable. When you see waves or spikes, it means the ground is shaking. Bigger waves = stronger shaking. The graph usually shows 5 minutes of continuous data, so you can see how seismic activity changes over time.

üåä Seismic Phases: P-waves and S-waves

When an earthquake occurs, it sends out different types of waves through the Earth. The two main types we detect are P-waves (Primary waves) and S-waves (Secondary waves).

üîµ P-waves (Primary)

‚ö°

Speed: Fastest (arrives first)

Type: Compression waves

Movement: Push-pull motion, like a slinky

Can travel through: Solids, liquids, and gases

Feels like: A sudden jolt or bump

üî¥ S-waves (Secondary)

üåä

Speed: Slower (arrives second)

Type: Shear waves

Movement: Side-to-side motion, like a rope

Can travel through: Solids only

Feels like: Rolling or swaying motion

üéØ Why This Matters:

By measuring the time difference between when P-waves and S-waves arrive, scientists can calculate how far away the earthquake occurred. This is why detecting both waves is so important for earthquake location!

üéØ What are "Picks"?

A "pick" is the exact moment when our AI system (PhaseNet) detects the arrival of a seismic wave (P-wave or S-wave) in the data. Think of it as marking the precise timestamp when an earthquake wave reached the monitoring station.

Why are picks important?

  • üìç Location: Combining picks from multiple stations helps pinpoint where the earthquake occurred
  • üìä Magnitude: The strength and characteristics of picks help estimate earthquake size
  • ‚è±Ô∏è Timing: Accurate picks enable precise calculations of wave travel times
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Early Warning: Fast, automatic picks can trigger earthquake warning systems
üìã Understanding the Picks Table:

Phase: Which type of wave (P or S)
Time: Exact moment the wave arrived
Confidence: How certain the AI is about this detection (higher is better)
Duration: How long the wave signal lasted
Trace ID: Which seismometer channel detected it

ü§ñ What is PhaseNet?

PhaseNet is an advanced artificial intelligence system specifically designed to detect seismic phases in earthquake data. It uses deep learning (a type of AI) to automatically identify P-waves and S-waves with high accuracy.

Why use AI for earthquake detection?

  • Speed: Analyzes data in seconds instead of minutes or hours
  • Accuracy: Can detect tiny earthquakes humans might miss
  • Consistency: Never gets tired or makes subjective judgments
  • Scale: Can process data from thousands of stations simultaneously

Our system uses PhaseNet to continuously analyze data from 21 global seismic stations, automatically detecting and cataloging seismic events as they happen around the world.

üìä Key Metrics Explained

üïê Processing Timestamp

The date and time when our system analyzed this data. Stations are processed in a continuous cycle.

‚è±Ô∏è Time Window

The 5-minute period of seismic data being displayed. Shows the start and end time of the recording.

üéØ Picks Detected

Number of seismic wave arrivals (P-waves and S-waves) identified by PhaseNet in this time window. Zero picks means no significant seismic activity was detected.

üì° Sampling Rate

How many data points the seismometer records per second (measured in Hz). Higher sampling rates (like 40 Hz or 100 Hz) provide more detailed measurements. Most stations use 20-100 Hz.
